# Is There a Market for Building a Business in This Area?

Short answer: **Yes.** The market for enterprise AI / AI Factory / agentic AI services is large, growing, and underserved relative to demand. There are real barriers (which create opportunity for providers who can overcome them).

---

## 1. Market size and growth

| Segment | Size / range | Growth (CAGR) |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Enterprise AI platforms** | ~$13B (2024) → ~$50B (2030) | ~28% |
| **AI as a Service** | ~$16B (2024) → ~$105B (2030) | ~36% |
| **AI services (broader)** | ~$113B opportunity through 2029 | ~43% |
| **Enterprise AI (overall)** | ~$94B opportunity through 2029 | ~54% |
| **Agentic AI (global)** | → ~$103B by 2032 | strong growth |

Sources: Verdantix, Grand View Research, Technavio, Deloitte, industry reports.

- **Geography:** North America is largest today; **Asia‑Pacific is fastest growing**, which fits an Australia/APAC-focused play (like Actualisation).
- **Buyers:** Large enterprises still dominate spend (~73% share), but SMB adoption is rising (e.g. >75% of APAC SMEs using at least one AI-enabled platform).

So the **area** (enterprise AI, AI Factory-style platforms, agentic solutions) is a **large and growing market**.

---

## 2. Demand for “AI Factory” / agentic solutions

- **~62%** of organisations are at least **experimenting** with AI agents.
- **~57%** already have AI agents **in production** (no longer just pilots).
- **~80%** of automation leaders plan to **accelerate AI agent investments** over 2025.
- **~75%** of companies have **invested in AI agents in the past year**.

Use cases are broadening: beyond customer service into **product development, marketing, supply chain**, and internal operations (scheduling, coaching, documents, etc.) — exactly the kind of work Actualisation-style “AI Factory + agents” address.

So there is **real demand** for what this type of business sells: central AI/data layer + multiple agents + implementation support.

---

## 3. The execution gap (why the market is underserved)

Many companies **want** AI/agents but **struggle to do it themselves**:

- **~98%** are experimenting with AI, but only **~26%** have moved beyond proof-of-concept to sustained value.
- **~50%** of enterprises report **delays, underperformance, or failure** in AI initiatives despite investment.
- Main barriers: **data readiness** (pipelines, quality, integration), **skills**, **governance**, and **organisational change** — not lack of interest.

Implication: there is a **persistent need for external help** — strategy, design, build, and run. That’s the core market for an “AI Factory / agentic” services business: **companies that want outcomes but can’t (or won’t) build it all in-house.**

---

## 4. Australia / APAC relevance

- **Australia:** Strong demand from financial services, telecoms, insurance, government. AI-capable consultancies (e.g. Accenture Australia) are growing; some AI deals in the **$50–100M+** range. Traditional consulting revenue is under pressure; **AI advisory and implementation** are where growth is.
- **APAC:** High adoption expectations (e.g. >75% SMEs using AI-enabled tools; 90% expect AI to be important in five years). Projections suggest **large economic benefit** from AI adoption across the region.

So building a business in **this area** in Australia or APAC is aligned with **where the market is growing** and where budgets are shifting.

---

## 5. Competitive landscape (why there’s still room)

- **Big players:** Accenture, Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC, and large tech vendors do strategy and implementation. They dominate **big**, multi-year enterprise deals.
- **Specialists:** Firms like Actualisation (120+ people, APAC) focus on **AI Factory + agentic** as a positioning. There are other regional and niche players.
- **Gap:** Many mid-market and industry-specific clients are **too small** for the largest players’ engagement model, or want **focused** AI/agent work rather than full-scale transformation. That leaves room for **smaller, focused** providers who can deliver:
  - AI Factory design and build (data layer, orchestration, security),
  - Agentic AI (custom agents, scheduling, coaching, documents, voice, etc.),
  - Industry or use-case focus (e.g. field services, retail, logistics).

So: **yes, there is competition, but the market is big enough and the “execution gap” large enough that there is still room for new entrants** who are clear on positioning (e.g. segment, industry, or offering).

---

## 6. Summary: is there a market?

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Is the **overall market** large and growing? | **Yes.** Enterprise AI / AI services / agentic AI are multi‑tens of billions and growing at high double-digit rates. |
| Is there **demand** for AI Factory / agentic solutions? | **Yes.** Most firms are experimenting or already running agents; many plan to invest more; use cases are expanding. |
| Can companies **do it themselves**? | **Often no.** Widespread experimentation but only a minority beyond POC; data, skills, and governance are real barriers. |
| Does that create **opportunity for providers**? | **Yes.** Demand for design, build, and run services that close the execution gap. |
| Is **Australia/APAC** a good region? | **Yes.** Fast growth, strong demand, and deals flowing to AI-focused players. |
| Is it **crowded**? | **Partly.** Big consultancies and some specialists exist, but mid-market and focused plays are still open. |

**Bottom line:** There **is** a market for building a business in this area. The opportunity is strongest for providers who can clearly define **who** they serve (e.g. mid-market, specific industries), **what** they deliver (e.g. AI Factory + first agents, or specific solutions like scheduling/coaching), and **how** they deliver (e.g. fixed-scope engagements, productised offerings, or retainers). The playbook in **BUILD_THEIR_BUSINESS_PLAYBOOK.md** is aimed at that.
